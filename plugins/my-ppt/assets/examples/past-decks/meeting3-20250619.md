Speaker notes:

- To answer the question of how ions distribute in a droplet. We can recall the knowledge of classical electromagnetic theory. Unlike metal, whose charge is distributed on surface, the mirror charge in loose dielectric is the same as the dense one, thus pushing the ions aways from the surface.
- Answering the question is not easy, the ions may have very different behavior because of the complex environment in liquid or solution.
- - Some experiments like sum frequency generation(和頻光譜) and second harmonic generation(二次諧波) shows that surface’s hydronium signal is enhanced.
- - While colloid research like zeta potential measurement claims that more hydroxide are concentrated on surface, even in acidic condition.
- In fact, the distribution of ions depends on the surface type and the number of cations and anions on surface, different ions will have different behavior.
- Top right: The molecular rationalization of this effect is as follows. Hydronium with its strongly positively charged hydrogens but weakly negatively charged oxygen a very good hydrogen bond donor but a poor acceptor. For this reason, in bulk water it tends to disrupt the locaisl hydrogen bond network. At the surface hydronium can, however, very efficiently be accommodated with an orientation with hydrogens pointing into the bulk and hydrogen bonded to neighbouring water molecules, while the oxygen sticks into the gas phase.

Speaker notes:

- Some early process on autoionization can be seen on this paper published in 2007 on PCCP. It used the modified POL3 model to run MD simulation, which is a 3 points model and very similar to the TIP3P model. It allows molecules to be polarized by local electric field and includes an additional energy term accounting for the dipole interaction.
- To compare with the DFT energy. The model parameters are re-fitted with an empirical functional B3LYP. Because of the limitation of computational resource, there are only 432 molecules in the box, and involves some manually placed Eigen, Zundel, and hydroxide. Its shows the trends that eigen is concentrated on surface, with free energy around minus point one three electric Volt per molecule. And the estimated pH value is around 4.8, which is rather acidic compared to pure water. And the MD study also shows no preference for hydroxide.
- The study also conducted an ab initio study using GGA functional with around 72 water molecules. They observed the same phenomenon that the cation remains at the surface and the anion settled in the bulk. They also did an experiment by introducing both ions. Although they will recombine in 5 picoseconds, they did form a metastable ion-pair before it.
- The lower right figure, we look at the first plot on a slab, the black dash line is the time when recombination happens. We can see that there is no obvious difference in slab system, therefore, no preference for forming an ion pair or keeping neutral. In the second plot, we can see that forming an ion pair will have a higher free energy.

Speaker notes:

- To explain this phenomenon in nanoscale, the author calculated the orientation order of the ions in the water. He comes up with two major reasons.
- The first is the orientation order makes entropy lower. In the upper left plot, we can see that most of the hydronium is facing towards the bubble. This helps the process thermodynamically.
- The second reason is the low coordination number of H bond network. The ions introduced in water will weaken the H bond network. The loose network will have higher enthalpy. And loot at the plot (b), in acidic condition, we can see that after resolving some molecules in the solution, the hydrogen bond network is strengthened. But in alkaline condition, there is no change after resolution process.
- As I discussed before, one possible explanation is because the hydronium is very weak at accepting hydrogen bonds, but the hydroxide can form a strong hydrogen bond network because it can accept up to 5 hydrogen.
